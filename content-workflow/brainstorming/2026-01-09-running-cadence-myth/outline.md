# Post Outline

## Title
"What Your Running Watch Isn't Telling You About Cadence"

## Subtitle
"Those color zones are comparing you to everyone else—not telling you what's right for your body"

## Structure

### Intro: The Orange Zone Anxiety
**Personal hook:**
- Looking at Garmin after a run
- Cadence shows orange zone again
- Feels like a warning: something's wrong with your form
- But is it really wrong?

**Problem setup:**
- Running watches display cadence in color-coded zones
- Orange/red feels like you're doing something wrong
- Creates anxiety about hitting arbitrary targets
- What are these colors actually measuring?

**The discovery:**
- Dug into Garmin's manual to find out
- Found the answer—and it's not what you'd expect

### Section 1: What the Colors Actually Mean
**The reveal: It's percentile rankings**

Garmin's cadence zones explained:
- Purple (>183 spm) = Top 5% of all Garmin users
- Blue (174-183 spm) = 70th-95th percentile
- Green (164-173 spm) = 30th-69th percentile ("average")
- Orange (153-163 spm) = 5th-29th percentile ("less experienced or slower")
- Red (<153 spm) = Bottom 5%

**The problem:**
- You're being compared to everyone else, not to what's optimal for YOU
- No adjustment for height, pace, or individual biomechanics
- The 180 myth embedded in the algorithm (purple starts at 183)

### Section 2: Garmin's Own Contradiction
**What Garmin admits in their manual:**

Direct quote: "More experienced runners tend to exhibit...higher cadence than less experienced runners."

**But immediately after:**
"However, taller runners typically have slightly slower cadences, longer strides, and slightly higher vertical oscillation."

**The irony:**
- They acknowledge height affects cadence
- Yet still color-code taller runners as "below average"
- No personalization despite knowing individual factors matter

### Section 3: What Research Actually Shows About Cadence
**Finding 1: 50% is just YOU**
- Research modeling cadence with speed, height, weight, age, experience explained only 50% of variation
- Other 50% is unmeasurable individual factors
- Elite runners vary wildly: 155 spm vs 203 spm, performing equally well

**Finding 2: Height/leg length matter enormously**
- Every inch of height = ~3 fewer steps per minute
- 6-foot runner naturally takes ~18 fewer steps/min than 5'6" runner
- Formula: `Cadence = -1.251 × Leg Length (cm) + 3.665 × Speed (m/s) + 254.858`
- Longer legs = lower leg stiffness = naturally lower cadence

**Finding 3: Speed drives cadence within the same person**
- Easy pace vs tempo vs sprint = different natural cadences
- Recent study (2025): experienced runners ranged 169-178 spm at different speeds
- Comparing cadences across paces makes no sense

**Finding 4: When cadence actually matters**
- Very low cadence (<153 spm) may increase injury risk
- Lower cadence = higher ground reaction forces
- BUT: 10% cadence increases don't improve economy for recreational runners
- Forcing changes to hit "green zone" won't make you faster or more efficient

### Section 4: The 180 Myth's Long Shadow
- Originated from 1984 Olympic distance runners study
- At peak competitive speed only
- NOT based on recreational runners
- NOT tested across different paces or body types
- Now embedded in Garmin's percentile thresholds (purple = 183+)
- Perpetuated without context

### Section 5: What This Means for You
**Stop chasing colors:**
- Orange zone doesn't mean you're doing something wrong
- Especially if you're tall, running easy pace, or built differently
- Colors rank you against others, not assess YOUR optimal cadence

**What to focus on instead:**
1. Avoid injury-risk territory (very low <153 spm)
2. Find what feels natural and sustainable for YOUR body
3. Accept that your cadence at easy pace will differ from race pace
4. Understand 50% of cadence is unique to you—unmeasurable

**Data literacy lesson:**
- Question how metrics are calculated
- Percentile rankings ≠ personal optimization
- Context always matters
- Your body's data is more important than population averages

### Conclusion
Your running watch gives you data. But understanding what that data actually measures—and doesn't measure—is the difference between useful information and unnecessary anxiety. The colors tell you where you rank compared to other runners, not whether your cadence is right for you.

Next time you see orange, remember: Garmin's own manual admits taller runners have slower cadences. The zones don't account for this. Don't let a population comparison convince you that your body is wrong.

## Key Messages
1. Color zones are percentile rankings, not personal optimization
2. Garmin acknowledges individual factors but doesn't adjust for them
3. 50% of cadence is unmeasurable individual variation
4. Height/leg length significantly affect natural cadence
5. Very low cadence may increase injury risk, but chasing "better" colors won't help
6. Data literacy = understanding what metrics actually measure vs what they claim
