## Sources Found

### Source 1: Time Course of Cardiorespiratory Adaptations
- **Type**: Scientific paper (Journal of Applied Physiology)
- **Link**: https://journals.physiology.org/doi/full/10.1152/japplphysiol.01152.2009
- **Key Findings**:
  - VO₂max increased within 3 weeks with further increases after 9 weeks of training
  - Half-time for adaptation rate was 13.8 days (8.3 training sessions)
  - Plasma volume expansion (~5%) occurs early, red blood cell volume changes lag behind (~2%)
  - Older adults showed +31% improvement, young adults +18%
- **Relevance**: Supports claim that fitness improvements happen over weeks/months, not days
- **How to cite in post**: "Research shows that cardiorespiratory fitness improvements begin within 3 weeks but continue developing over months" with link

### Source 2: Structured Training Effects (12 weeks)
- **Type**: Scientific paper (PMC)
- **Link**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6492765/
- **Key Findings**:
  - All exercise types showed similar improvements after 12 weeks
  - VO₂max increased ≥11%, maximal heart rate ≥8%, test duration ≥14%
  - Confirms medium-term training timeline
- **Relevance**: Supports the "trust the long game" message - 12 weeks is typical timeframe
- **How to cite in post**: Mention in context of "why weekly summaries miss the picture"

### Source 3: Periodized vs Non-Periodized Training
- **Type**: Scientific review article
- **Link**: Multiple studies, primary: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7590922/
- **Key Findings**:
  - Structured periodized training produces greater improvements than unstructured training
  - Quote: "periodized training elicited greater improvements in endurance performance than nonperiodized training, highlighting the importance of planned training structure"
  - Progressive and individualized overload reduces injury occurrence
- **Relevance**: Directly supports your observation that structured training (Tactic Method) showed clear changes in your data
- **How to cite in post**: "Studies confirm that structured, periodized training programs are more effective than unstructured running" with link

### Source 4: Fitness Tracker Adherence and Motivation
- **Type**: Systematic review and meta-analysis (The Lancet Digital Health, 2024)
- **Link**: https://www.thelancet.com/journals/landig/article/PIIS2589-7500(24)00139-0/fulltext
- **Key Findings**:
  - Users often exhibit poor long-term adherence to fitness tracking devices
  - Wearable trackers increase daily steps but not moderate-to-vigorous physical activity
  - Self-monitoring is most effective behavioral change technique
  - Effectiveness improves when integrated into comprehensive, personally tailored interventions
  - Adherence influenced by "individual's recognition of long-term benefits"
- **Relevance**: Supports your approach of creating comprehensive dashboard focused on long-term patterns rather than relying on app feedback alone
- **How to cite in post**: "Research shows fitness trackers work best when users focus on long-term benefits rather than daily notifications" with link

### Source 5: Wearable Activity Tracker Behavior Change
- **Type**: Scientific paper (PMC, 2024)
- **Link**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11555328/
- **Key Findings**:
  - Users cited "sense of achievement, validation, and motivational feedback" as helping adherence
  - Discontinuation factors: professional workload, charging annoyance, lost motivation over time
  - Wearable tracker usage associated with short-term gains in physical activity
- **Relevance**: Context for why apps focus on daily engagement vs long-term perspective
- **How to cite in post**: Background context, possibly not directly cited
